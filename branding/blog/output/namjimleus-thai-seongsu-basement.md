# Namjimleus (남짐릇): Thai Food in a Seongsu Basement

The stairs go down. That's the first thing. You're walking through Seongsu, past the third café with exposed concrete and a line for oat milk lattes, and then you descend into a small Thai restaurant that smells like lemongrass and fish sauce and something vaguely fermented. Good fermented. Namjimleus has built a quiet reputation among locals who know Thai food—the kind of place where regulars order the same dish three times a week because it genuinely tastes better here than elsewhere in the city.

I went without a reservation. There was a wait. Learn from my mistakes.

---

## What's Actually Worth Ordering

**Gopchang Tom Yum Rice Noodles (곱창똠얌쌀국수) — ₩18,000**

This is the one. Gopchang is intestine—beef intestine—and in a tom yum broth, it does something interesting. The broth carries that familiar tom yum sourness and heat, but the gopchang adds a deep, almost muddy umami underneath it. Not muddy in a bad way. Muddy in a way that makes you drink the broth after you've finished the noodles. The texture of the intestine is soft but with some chew. If you're squeamish about offal, this is a reasonable place to try it for the first time because the broth does most of the heavy lifting.

**Soft-Shell Crab Panang Curry (뿌님팟퐁커리) — ₩29,500**

More expensive, and worth thinking about before you order it. The panang is creamy, coconut-forward, slightly sweet—classic in that sense. The soft-shell crab soaks it up well, and the batter stays together better than I expected given the sauce. It's the dish that photographs well and also tastes good, which isn't always a given. At ₩29,500, it's a treat order, not an everyday one.

**Khao Kha Mu (pig's feet rice) — ₩16,000**

I'll be honest—this was the quietest dish at the table. Tender pork, well-braised, rice underneath, the whole setup. The seasoning is subtle. Balanced, technically. But if you want bold, go with one of the others and add this as a side order if there are a few of you sharing.

---

## One Thing to Know

Some people leave here feeling like the flavors have been adjusted for Korean palates. I think that's a fair read. Compared to tom yum soup standards in Sukhumvit Bangkok, Namjimleus's version prioritizes balance over heat intensity. The heat is present but not confrontational. The sourness is there but not aggressive. This isn't a complaint, exactly—the food is genuinely good—but if you've spent time in Thailand and you want something that makes your sinuses run, you may want to request it spicier upfront. The kitchen can work with you.

---

## The Space

Basement. Low light. The interior leans into the Bangkok-side-street aesthetic without going overboard about it. Wood, warm tones, no fluorescent overhead lighting to flatten everything. It's small enough that every table feels occupied at once, which is part of why it fills up. Go early for lunch or book ahead for dinner.

---

## Getting There

Three minutes from Seongsu Station—exit the station, walk, look for the stairs going down. Open Monday through Friday from 11 AM to 2:30 PM and 5 to 9 PM. Weekends they start at noon, same closing time. No gap days. During lunch hours on weekdays, the wait without a reservation averages 30–40 minutes; dinner reservations typically book out 3–5 days in advance on weekends. Book a reservation. I said it already and I'll say it again.
