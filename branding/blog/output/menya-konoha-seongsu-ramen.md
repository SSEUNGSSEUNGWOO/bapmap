# Menya Konoha — Ramen Without the Performance

Seongsu on a weekday lunch: a woman at the table next to me is eating alone, reading a book, working through a bowl of ramen like it's the most normal thing in the world. No phone propped against the glass. No overhead shot. Just a person eating good noodles. Menya Konoha is a small ramen restaurant in Seongsu where this actually happens — locals come here to eat, not to perform. That's the vibe.

This place sits on a side street a few blocks from the main Seongsu drag, away from the oat milk latte crowd. It's small, clean, and serious about broth.

---

## What You're Eating

The **Ssangmun-dong Ramen** is the signature, and it earns that label. The broth is pork-based, deep and slightly cloudy — closer to Fukuoka tonkotsu in spirit but not as heavy. You're not drinking liquid fat here. The flavor is more round than aggressive: salted umami, a little sweetness underneath, no single note dominating. The chashu sits on top in two or three thick slices, tender enough to fall apart if you look at it wrong. Noodles are the chewy, medium-thickness type that hold up through the whole bowl without going soft on you.

The **Spicy Tonkatsu Ramen** swaps the base for something with more heat and richer pork fat. The egg — soft-boiled, jammy yolk — is better here than in most bowls I've had in the city. I'd order this one if you want more weight to the meal.

Get the **Gyoza**. I know everyone says that, but it's true. They're pan-fried on one side, skins thin enough to have some bite, and the filling is all pork and chive — clean, savory, no filler taste. They come in a small plate of six and take maybe four minutes to disappear.

Ramen runs ₩10,000 to ₩14,000 per bowl, gyoza slightly less. For Seongsu, where a salad can run ₩18,000, this is a fair deal.

---

## One Honest Note

The space is compact. Eight tables, maybe less. On a weekend afternoon, there's a wait. It's not the kind of wait where someone hands you a buzzer and you browse the area — it's the kind where you stand near the door and hope the couple finishing up isn't planning to linger. Locals know to show up right at 11:30 AM when they open for lunch. You get seated immediately, the kitchen is ready, and you're done before the lunch wave hits. That's the move.

There's no late-night option. The restaurant closes at 8:30 PM sharp, and they mean it.

---

## Who I'd Bring Here

This is the one I'd take someone on their first trip to Seoul who keeps saying they want to "eat like a local." Not because it's a performance of local eating, but because it actually is local eating. No English menu that I noticed — point at what you want or use a translation app, staff will figure it out.

It's also a solid solo lunch spot. Counter seating, book, bowl. Done in 30 minutes if you want to be.

---

## Getting There

**Seongsu Station** (Line 2), Exit 3. Walk about 14 minutes — mostly flat, through the residential blocks before the neighborhood gets trendy. You'll pass a few dry cleaners and a hardware store. You're going the right direction.

Open every day from 11:30 AM to 8:30 PM. No reservation system. First come, first seated.
