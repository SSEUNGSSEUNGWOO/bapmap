# Namjimleus (남짐릇), Seongsu: Thai Food Worth Finding Underground

The staircase going down is easy to miss. That's fine. Once you find it, you're in a low-lit room with warm wood tones, Thai script on the walls, and the smell of lemongrass doing something useful for the first time all day.

Namejimleus is a Thai restaurant under a Seongsu street, three minutes from the station on Exit 3 or 4. The neighborhood is lousy with specialty coffee and aesthetic brunch spots. This place serves intestines in tom yum broth and charges you less than twenty bucks for it. It's been operating here for several years now, long enough to have built a quiet reputation among people who know where to look. Respect.

---

## What I Ate

**Gopchang Tom Yum Rice Noodles (곱창똠얌쌀국수) — ₩18,000**

This is the dish people come back for, and I get it. The broth is the point. It's sour and funky and has real depth — the kind that suggests the kitchen actually built it from scratch rather than opened something. They make the stock fresh daily with lemongrass, galangal, and tom yum paste, and you can taste that labor in every spoonful. The gopchang (intestines) add a richness that tips the whole bowl past "interesting" into "I'm finishing this." The rice noodles hold up. No complaints there.

One caveat that's worth naming: a few regulars have noted that the seasoning leans toward what Korean palates tend to prefer — less aggressive heat, slightly rounder edges than you'd get in Bangkok. I didn't find it offensive, but if you're expecting full-volume Thai spice, calibrate accordingly.

**Soft-shell Crab Panang Curry (뿌님팟퐁커리) — ₩29,500**

Expensive for this neighborhood, justified for what arrives. The curry is thick and coconut-forward without being sweet, and the soft-shell crab sits in it with more structural integrity than you'd expect. The shell fries up crisp enough to hold its own against the sauce. It's a generous portion. Two people splitting this with rice makes sense.

**Khao Kha Mu — Pig's Feet Rice**

Quieter than the other two. The pork is braised until it surrenders completely, and the seasoning is subtle — soy, star anise, something low and sweet underneath. It won't grab you the way the tom yum does, but it's the dish I'd order again on a day I wanted something that didn't require my full attention.

---

## The Space

Small. That matters. The room seats maybe thirty people and the atmosphere is specific enough to feel intentional — dark enough to be cozy, not so dark you can't see your food. It reads less like a Korean interpretation of a Thai restaurant and more like someone just liked how Thai restaurants actually look and built that.

It fills up. I went without a reservation and waited. That was my fault.

---

## The Honest Part

If you want Thai food that punches you in the face with fish sauce and bird's eye chili, this might not be the right stop. The kitchen has clearly made choices about where to land on the authenticity-versus-accessibility dial, and it lands closer to the middle than some places will. That's not necessarily a problem — the food is good — but it's worth knowing before you go in with specific expectations.

---

## Getting There

**Subway:** Seongsu Station (성수역), Exit 3 or 4 — about a three-minute walk. Look for the basement staircase sign at street level; the entrance descends below the main street.

**Hours:** Monday through Friday, 11am–2:30pm and 5–9pm. Saturday and Sunday, noon–3:30pm and 5–9pm.

**Reservations:** Make one. The room is small and they fill it. Walking in works sometimes, but sometimes means standing outside doing nothing useful.
