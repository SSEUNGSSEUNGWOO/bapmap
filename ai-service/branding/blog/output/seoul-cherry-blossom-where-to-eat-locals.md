# Cherry Blossom Season in Seoul: Where Locals Eat After

Cherry blossom season in Seoul peaks for 7 to 10 days in late March or early April—the exact window shifts annually depending on weather—but the crowds and the hunger afterward are constant. If you're planning a walk through Seoul's blooming neighborhoods and wondering where to eat when the photos are done, skip the overpriced cafes near Gyeongbokgung and convenience store picnic spreads on the Han River. This guide is for the hours after, when you're tired from crowds and genuinely hungry. Whether you've walked Yeouido Hangang Park or Seokchon Lake, here's where locals actually eat instead of where tourist guides point you.

---

## Near Yeouido Hangang Park

### 1. Jinju House — Old-school noodles from a basement that's been doing this longer than most restaurants in Seoul have existed

![](https://places.googleapis.com/v1/places/ChIJg3mO-z2ffDURxBvzNdq99p4/photos/AU_ZVEFI-aZ24_1BYuJBqt9FR5VkyLFGgAWImoUeZTw0umkTOxsRINRGJTp8odCylw_hcG53D5Yf7ruIuRYc-dZB1LyGvsvg7HTk6zpYHsMWfZP2-qhD9yOVb_THHX2GBJpEVlVXuoWDuIe3hggtRrhAu5FpKjt2GDXhw8_ZC6edCxhK5VEOcFcDAQF5c69RgXx0iEhp1mp4W7QiLRLYlcsXw6z5CNyTVH6bRdizuOah_vKSv08U4T4CIYag6TrPWh3KBWmJz1RnhJBIBO1xPPZC3PhaoewiCb7G79-TcKQQRX2GSRtMb8tgDrdl4d8Dzvn6g84kJ1y36kUdxoX-BrHQV1jCW50-ptUPHDQ6OM5rU0RPoPDJknaVP9yIg0ciZcut93RSgrv4APUQDi76QbfFoaSK8Gr3ku3qNJP3SDHwcBSwLFuc/media?maxWidthPx=800&key=AIzaSyBE2cwdc_b0H934UaYurklIc7toYDXhGGQ)


After a long walk through Yeouido's blossom crowds, Jinju House is the kind of place you want to land in. It's downstairs, it's unpretentious, and it's serious about exactly two things: konguksu and mandu. The cold soybean noodle soup is thick, nutty, and made without added sugar—a completely different thing from what most visitors expect when they hear "cold soup." The broth is blended from soybeans and served ice-cold over wheat noodles, and in the right weather, it genuinely resets you. A bowl of konguksu costs around ₩9,000 to ₩12,000, which is standard for Seoul but a relief if you've been eating near tourist zones. The mandu are just as good—hand-folded, dense with filling, and consistently praised as some of the best in the country by people who have eaten a lot of dumplings. If you're colder from the walk than expected, order the guksu with chicken instead. The place closes at 8 PM on weekdays and 7 PM on Saturdays, and it doesn't open Sundays, so plan accordingly.

**What to order:** Konguksu (cold bean noodle soup), Mandu, Guksu with Chicken

**Price:** ₩9,000–₩15,000 per person

**Getting there:** Yeouinaru Station, 14-minute walk

**Hours:** Mon–Fri 10:00 AM – 8:00 PM | Sat 10:00 AM – 7:00 PM | Closed Sunday

**More info:** bapmap.com/spots/jinju-house

### 2. Hwamok Sundaeguk — The offal soup spot that Yeongdeungpo office workers don't really want you to know about

![](https://places.googleapis.com/v1/places/ChIJqcae6D-ffDURyG0vP-k0xfc/photos/AU_ZVEEuxf3Qc0E0pTgNR1FreW4cRDEgd9Uo0agCHFbYZAE0HCIUhO2QkD4UXB5SzJG7nDuiwTxM8g8eEne3vG-dVmzUSa9B5M968eoDTMhsNpjdNxhPaswzJOkzaw9IPZHYzJlhYWfwr6Y0SAe_AMRs4s2TjFan9sO8fKy-3tDi2c4f43d1WUgjKADXlZY-r8KPa5qksiaJQ13Su62qf4lZoeOR0ZreCMCGMLdttFeVg9mYjiZvRvSjLhcYPct_u63TQ-uyd_twSzMdAVVKXD918L9H2P-2TtB9w2mabygFVzz5w6jd9_abalVy3l7DGQxa650O89wpwZtOAEYm5ODt71jyRaybPPXVua5Iz_e1fJFYWdeEKFZlHVvFZIUQd7LHXZbyVCy5l90XtVfyTYnUVRpCLL8tDHlPPWXlDzBiSeF9fzDd0-B3GbfNjg7OMl_a/media?maxWidthPx=800&key=AIzaSyBE2cwdc_b0H934UaYurklIc7toYDXhGGQ)


If Jinju House is full or you're after something warmer and more substantial, Hwamok Sundaeguk is a short walk away and serves a completely different meal. Sundaeguk is pork offal soup—blood sausage, intestines, organ meat, thick opaque broth. That description either appeals to you or it doesn't, but if you're curious about it, this is a decent place to try it for the first time. The broth is rich and savory without being aggressively spicy, and the portion is filling enough that you won't need to eat again for hours. A bowl runs roughly ₩8,000 to ₩10,000, making it one of Seoul's most affordable post-walk meals. Order the sundaeguk with extra intestines if you want the full experience; skip the add-on if you're still testing the waters. Note the split hours: they close in the afternoon between 2:30 PM and 5:00 PM, so if you're arriving after a late blossom walk, aim to get there before 2 or after 5. Sundays are closed.

**What to order:** Sundaeguk, Sundaeguk with extra intestines

**Price:** ₩8,000–₩12,000 per person

**Getting there:** Yeouinaru Station, 13-minute walk

**Hours:** Mon–Sat 10:30 AM – 2:30 PM, 5:00 PM – 10:00 PM | Closed Sunday

**More info:** bapmap.com/spots/hwamok-sundaeguk

## Near Seokchon Lake

### 3. Prettit Lotte World Mall — Fresh pretzels when you need something in hand and you're not ready for a full meal

![](https://jiaunorookzwlopxpppk.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/spot-images/prettit-lotte-world-mall.webp)


Seokchon Lake's blossom walk ends right near Lotte World Mall, and Prettit is in the basement level of that mall. It's not a restaurant—it's a pretzel counter, and it's exactly right for a particular post-walk moment: you're tired, you want something warm, and sitting down for a full meal sounds like too much commitment. The pretzels are made in-house daily using French butter, which is why the smell hits you before you see the stall. The almond cream cheese pretzel is their most popular item and worth trying if it's available. Salt butter is the safer, more classic call. It pairs well with coffee from anywhere nearby if you need a sit-down moment before deciding what's next. Keep in mind there are no reviews on record for this location yet, so treat it as a practical stop rather than a destination.

**What to order:** Almond Cream Cheese Pretzel, Salt Butter Pretzel, Cinnamon Pretzel

**Price:** Check in store

**Getting there:** Jamsil Station, 7-minute walk

**Hours:** Check with Lotte World Mall directly for current hours

**More info:** bapmap.com/spots/prettit-lotte-world-mall

### 4. Jamsil Mosori — Pork BBQ that goes beyond the standard cuts, for evenings when the day calls for something more

![](https://places.googleapis.com/v1/places/ChIJsf5kYvulfDURyXea0zMs1Cg/photos/AU_ZVEE-xm-3OHJFe9CAnvu77P6IJ09p15YCJ2Z3tYrGu_5BX1lJPj1QgjaBBSfaH50tTtQVsPmjbHyNNuQuQLfE9q71NWoi8qiXQtVcmid1tlIWulFTznQeIZsGE1aEC0aXxORjQQP3qa4Er7r-99QFluXQMqJsqiHrpaxq6gUevghVxhiYP_fDdKYaA4FG6Z9gwZ2FTZaEWPy2w1C0swULnNS6l5KwgBg5OKhUHZHcZxpC_RFIwlMKj2Y_XXaAqP75GHXtoy7sBobyxxEtRqz-Rob0YzS9Le2-y40ZDVQmuHxpBA/media?maxWidthPx=800&key=AIzaSyBE2cwdc_b0H934UaYurklIc7toYDXhGGQ)


Jamsil Mosori is an evening option—it opens at 5 PM daily and runs until 11 PM, which makes it a natural end-of-day choice after a full afternoon at Seokchon Lake. The focus here is specialty pork cuts, meaning the parts of the pig that most tourist-facing BBQ restaurants don't bother with: cheek meat, collar, and other cuts that regulars order without needing to check the menu. The quality is consistently noted as good for the price, and the staff manages the grill attentively, which matters when you're dealing with cuts that are easy to overcook. It's a 14-minute walk from Jamsil Station, so not immediately convenient, but if you're planning ahead and want a proper dinner rather than a quick bite, it's worth the extra distance. Only five reviews so far, so expectations should be calibrated accordingly—but the rating is high.

**What to order:** Specialty pork cuts (특수부위), Samgyeopsal, Korean BBQ assortment

**Price:** Check in store

**Getting there:** Jamsil Station, 14-minute walk

**Hours:** Daily 5:00 PM – 11:00 PM

**More info:** bapmap.com/spots/jamsil-mosori
