# Where to Eat in Myeongdong: 4 Local Restaurants Beyond Street Food

Myeongdong gets a bad rap for being all corn dogs and overpriced street snacks. Fair — but if you know where to look, there are proper sit-down restaurants here where Seoul office workers, students, and families spend their lunch hours instead. These aren't places full of tourists comparing Instagram photos. The restaurants on this list have been doing one thing reliably well for years, sometimes decades, and the people eating there are mostly not on a tour itinerary. They're here because they've been earning repeat customers through consistency, not through marketing deals. Here's where to actually eat.

---

## 1. Myeongdong Kyoja — Knife-cut noodles and dumplings done so consistently it hurts

Since 1966, Myeongdong Kyoja has run one of the shortest menus in the neighborhood — nearly sixty years and counting. That's not a limitation — it's the whole point. When a kitchen has been making the same three dishes for nearly six decades, something closer to mastery tends to happen. The restaurant fills up by noon most days with a mix of office workers and repeat customers who've been coming for years.

**What to order:** Start with the **mandu** — the dumplings are the signature and they earn it. The **kalguksu** (knife-cut noodle soup) is the kind of thing that makes sense of Korean comfort food if you're new to it. Come in summer? Get the **kongguksu**, a cold soybean broth noodle that is genuinely refreshing in a way that nothing on the street stalls outside will be.

There will be a line. It moves faster than it looks. Most dishes cost between ₩8,000–₩12,000 (roughly $6–$9 USD).

## 2. Ranju Kalmyeon — Chinese knife-cut noodles that have nothing to do with what you're expecting

Myeongdong has no shortage of places borrowing Chinese aesthetics to sell something vague and tourist-facing. Ranju Kalmyeon is not that. Operating since the late 1990s, this specialist focuses on dosamyeon — a Shanxi-style knife-cut noodle made by shaving dough directly off a block with a blade. The result is thick in the middle, thin at the edges, and genuinely chewy in a way that mass-produced noodles never are. You'll notice the difference immediately.

**What to order:** The **Seafood Stir-fried Dosamyeon** is the move — fresh seafood, wok heat, and those noodles doing exactly what they're supposed to do. If you want something broth-based, the **Samson Seafood Jjamppong Dosamyeon** delivers a spicy, clean broth with the same textural payoff. Skip anything on the menu that doesn't involve the knife-cut noodles. That's what you're here for. Expect to spend around ₩10,000–₩13,000 per dish ($7.50–$10 USD).

It's a smaller room and fills up. Go a little before or after peak lunch hours if you can.

## 3. Dongwon Restaurant — Pork spine soup for when you want to eat like someone who actually lives here

Nine minutes from the subway puts you just far enough from the tourist corridor that the crowd changes entirely. Dongwon Restaurant has been serving **gamjatang** — pork spine soup — to the people who work and live around Myeongdong for long enough that it doesn't need to advertise. The broth is deep and fermented-spicy, the kind of thing that takes hours of braising to arrive at. The meat comes off the bone without much effort. This is the kind of place where regulars have their own preferred tables.

**What to order:** The **gamjatang** is the reason to come. Order it, get rice, and don't overthink it. The **soondae** (Korean blood sausage) is a solid addition if you're eating with someone else. Unlimited broth and potato refills are available for a small extra charge (usually ₩2,000–₩3,000 more), which, at these prices, is a reasonable deal. A full bowl of gamjatang runs around ₩7,000–₩9,000 ($5–$7 USD).

Closed Sundays. Worth noting before you make the walk.

## 4. Butai Act 2 — A Japanese noodle counter that Seoulites queue for on weekday lunch breaks

No English signage out front. No tourist foot traffic pulling people in off the street. By 11:45 AM on a weekday, there are already ten groups waiting. They're almost all Korean, and they all came specifically for the mazesoba. The restaurant has developed a devoted following since opening in the early 2010s, with locals treating it as a reliable weekday lunch destination.

**What to order:** The **mazesoba** is the anchor of the menu — dry mixed noodles with spicy sauce, minced pork, egg, and vegetables. You toss it yourself at the table and eat it fast before it clumps. It's the kind of dish that has a real following for a reason, usually priced around ₩9,000–₩11,000 ($7–$8 USD). The **hirekkatsu** (pork fillet cutlet, slight pink center, served with lemon) is excellent if you're eating with someone and want to share. The **katsu sandwich** is worth knowing about if you're grabbing a quick lunch solo, hovering around ₩8,000 ($6 USD).

Operates on a split schedule — lunch service ends at 2:00 PM, dinner picks back up at 5:00 PM. Closed Sundays.

## Before You Go — A Few Practical Notes

None of these places take reservations, so timing matters. Aim to arrive before noon or after 1:30 PM to avoid the longest waits. Most of these restaurants don't have English menus, so having your phone ready with photos or the Korean names of dishes helps. All four are within a 15-minute walk of Myeongdong Station, making them accessible without much navigation beyond that. If you're visiting on a Sunday, Dongwon and Butai Act 2 will be closed, so plan accordingly.
