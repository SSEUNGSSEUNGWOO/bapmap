# Thai Food in a Basement, and It's Actually Worth It: Namjimleus (남짐릇)

Seongsu has a café problem. Every third storefront is selling oat milk lattes in a beautifully renovated factory space, and honestly, good for them — but at some point you need actual food. Namjimleus is three minutes from the station, one floor underground, and it's the kind of place where the food does the talking. A 4.7 rating from 18 reviews isn't a lot of data points, but 18 people who bothered to write something down? That's a reliable signal.

---

## What to Order

Start with the **Gopchang Tom Yum Rice Noodles (곱창똠얌쌀국수, ₩18,000)**. This is the dish people mention first, and for good reason. The broth is genuinely umami-heavy — the kind that tastes like someone was paying attention the whole time it was cooking. The gopchang (intestines) add depth and a slight chew that works surprisingly well with the sour-spicy tom yum base. If you're new to gopchang, this is an approachable entry point. If you already like it, you'll get why this combo makes sense.

The **Soft-shell Crab Panang Curry (뿌님팟퐁커리, ₩29,500)** is the splurge item, and based on the reviews, it earns it. Creamy, rich, well-seasoned — the kind of curry that makes you slow down. Soft-shell crab can go wrong easily (soggy, overcooked, lost in the sauce), but the praise here is consistent enough that it seems like they've figured it out. Worth ordering if you're with someone to split it.

The **Khao Kha Mu (pig's feet rice)** rounds things out if you want something more grounded. Tender meat, subtle seasoning, nothing aggressive — it's the dish you order when you want Thai comfort food rather than anything that's going to demand your attention. Good balance.

One honest caveat: some people find the food slightly toned down for Korean palates — less punch than you'd get at a place aiming for strict Thai authenticity. That's not a dealbreaker, and it's worth knowing going in. The flavors are still there. They're just not going to knock you over.

---

## The Space

Basement, warm lighting, décor that actually references Thailand rather than just gesturing at it. It's small, which means it gets intimate fast — in a good way on a date, potentially annoying if you're a group of five hoping to spread out. The vibe leans more neighborhood restaurant than Instagram moment, which in Seongsu is either a relief or something to note depending on why you're there.

---

## Practical Info

**Getting there:** Seongsu Station (성수역), Exit 3. Three-minute walk, then head down to basement level. You're not looking for a big sign — just follow the address and look for the stairs.

**Hours:**
- Monday–Friday: 11:00 AM–2:30 PM, 5:00–9:00 PM
- Saturday–Sunday: 12:00–3:30 PM, 5:00–9:00 PM

There's a lunch break between service windows, so time it right. Don't show up at 4:00 PM expecting to eat.

**Reservations:** Make one. I showed up without a reservation and there was a wait. The place is small enough that walk-ins are a gamble, especially on weekends and Friday evenings. Save yourself the trouble and book ahead.

**Price range:** The noodles are ₩18,000, the curry is ₩29,500. Mid-range for Seongsu, reasonable for the quality. Not the cheapest Thai food in Seoul, but you're not paying for atmosphere markup either.

---

## Who This Is For

A solid pick for a date — the lighting helps and the food gives you something to talk about. Also works well for two people who want a proper sit-down lunch near Seongsu without ending up at a sandwich place. Solo diners should be fine at lunch; dinner might feel a bit cozier than you want if it's packed. Not ideal for large groups just because of the size constraints.

If you're doing a Seongsu afternoon — browsing the area, maybe hitting a café or two — this is a real meal worth building your timing around.
