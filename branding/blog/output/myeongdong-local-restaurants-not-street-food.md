# Best Local Restaurants in Myeongdong Beyond Street Food

Myeongdong gets a bad rap for being all corn dogs and overpriced street snacks. Fair — but if you know where to look, there are four proper sit-down restaurants here that locals and Seoulites have been going to for decades, with some operating continuously since the 1960s. These picks aren't ranked by Instagram appeal or proximity to the main strip. They're ranked by the simple fact that locals keep showing up, which in a neighborhood this saturated with tourist bait, means something. Here's where to actually eat.

---

## 1. Myeongdong Kyoja — The noodle institution that has earned its queue

![](https://places.googleapis.com/v1/places/ChIJW7FBDfCifDURHTpisLbVUH0/photos/AU_ZVEGREfrcSGD32H2ByaW_q4ewKiR0OMqgMM135-CsoKlPL9iBtEwjgmIAnDiIIy7NijuFBGdwUXx8KyumitwPPw8TwL3OPI6KQGFPjlCo66GCxQ5IzvowOWVfzGYwbXZ2nrVC_GvS74bhCLBFEJXGnJhERk0M6Yampdp8H6tP9QvcpiRrGtpzkR0wWhr6CN1TzX0aOek9byp7eR1b9Iy5tpZNaZ7jK_LIerCGiVV9dbhY2tmZBIByHGACs1-VkbfdgygUlpYiBmjz91nNO7T9ppOvGtJBVRcZ34Oaqfi-IXtRow/media?maxWidthPx=800&key=AIzaSyBE2cwdc_b0H934UaYurklIc7toYDXhGGQ)


If you only eat one sit-down meal in Myeongdong, this is the one. Myeongdong Kyoja has been running the same tight menu since 1966 — kalguksu, mandu, kongguksu in summer — and executing it with the kind of consistency that turns a restaurant into a landmark. The line typically wraps around the block during peak lunch hours (noon to 2 PM), but the operation is fast, with seats turning over within 15 to 20 minutes. The broth is exactly what you want after a morning of walking.

**What to order:** Kalguksu (knife-cut noodle soup) is the anchor dish — thick, hand-cut noodles in a clean chicken broth with a depth that takes time to build. A bowl costs around $8–10. Get the mandu (dumplings) on the side for $3–4. If you're visiting in summer, the kongguksu (chilled soybean noodles) is worth ordering on its own for $7–9.

**Price:** $$ · Myeongdong Station, 3 min walk · Open daily 10:30 AM – 9:00 PM

## 2. Ranju Kalmyeon — Shanxi-style knife-cut noodles that have nothing to do with Chinese takeout

![](https://places.googleapis.com/v1/places/ChIJO6g-RvGifDURx_vXYokm50I/photos/AU_ZVEEz6IvY7F4GZ3nHdg7c0OMombvayuZLL6ZRlYorQXZQ1rRoWjfFoQ0Q8zkh9S7K31Kspgnm88kFJbXtGkXVj5TZNPDdjUwC3L0j1gukFwwk3yPCm_e5NzaAnD3iDkoUDM1dpzxW4fRKl9N-CY-u3VC8z0g3p7MUyoiBhpkXa1ZAVDYxCN3P80ZC2es_CePtVgqKQu7MncoW-j_JhCd90iKtmjbN620Dd9rJP3Ttb-N_LQxOU6-Y7NQ5aGsu1cdrNf0-DDvCw023-215zID-PYjBO0e3wUkn1FISMW8ep5L_96CSSee6tra2CSsf2qJ3-lTFyAEiTYMkzUSlE9zfo-34-nb7G0CVX8YYCVtGbydS2IFJvTG2RGvPYoPDr6OKv-hipZWfU-Qmmab6hLCdsK1eHIjdyE68eKLkarXuVlwl24laoiis4oQNdu7nG2qO/media?maxWidthPx=800&key=AIzaSyBE2cwdc_b0H934UaYurklIc7toYDXhGGQ)


Myeongdong has no shortage of Chinese-Korean restaurants doing serviceable jjajangmyeon. Ranju Kalmyeon is doing something categorically different. The noodles here are dosakmyeon — a Shanxi-style knife-cut noodle shaved directly off a block of dough, thick in the middle and thin at the edges, with a chew that no machine-made noodle can replicate. It's been drawing a steady crowd for years without much fanfare, which is usually the right sign.

**What to order:** The Seafood Stir-fried Dosakmyeon is the signature — fresh seafood, wok heat, those noodles — running about $12–14. If you want something broth-based and spicy, the Samson Seafood Jjamppong Dosakmyeon ($11–13) delivers a sharp, clean heat that holds up against the thick noodles without getting muddy. Don't come expecting standard Korean-Chinese. The whole point here is the texture of the noodle itself.

**Price:** $$ · Myeongdong Station, 5 min walk · Open daily 11:00 AM – 9:30 PM

## 3. Dongwon Restaurant — A gamjatang spot that rewards the extra nine minutes of walking

![](https://places.googleapis.com/v1/places/ChIJxb7At-aifDURwrC7QqInp10/photos/AU_ZVEGLRo9r97IqanHcH_wrwQMRFoNpbu-LUVKNyH7YRAZpuKNtgcaNNuTUlvfv2zAh3aG3yPY8mJ_0qVrzrgvQqi12jjvhLpira2jE8GPLhy97Zja4lGXbUer97LUknZZtH4VLHEomkzbZjFNurhlOPiWcAmDphjky9Ut4joeyvxSu8ui84RguTNsERVxozpuOsVO2kOSFcISPf-1nwaOsTH5YpFo0I0EOF6XchgnZBAU091zkggxzSLyxVYLc5ryNq8yQ0uAEiTwpQtrvbrBZ7T92pUFJiI3FhNZREoed8Mrpsa-01H3K4MbXpZpXMrgo_yr9aMTt_leFQMCdtqmfAFe57SG7C7BrXmeFbRzwcNwI2cp-Z0fwYCZwNGQcxs15tCTVuwkHyIxc2D5IY8dqFwQNNZmywTayW7OQDHn_h20-Gh5Z/media?maxWidthPx=800&key=AIzaSyBE2cwdc_b0H934UaYurklIc7toYDXhGGQ)


Nine minutes from the subway is nine minutes past where most tourists stop. That gap is exactly what makes Dongwon Restaurant worth finding. The neighborhood shifts, the crowds thin out, and you end up in a straightforward Korean restaurant serving gamjatang — pork spine soup — to people who actually live and work nearby. A bowl of gamjatang costs around $9–12, making it significantly cheaper than the central Myeongdong options. The regulars are consistent, and that counts for more than a spike of first-time visitors.

**What to order:** Gamjatang, full stop. The broth is low-and-slow braised, the pork falls off the bone without much encouragement, and it pairs well with soju if you're eating in the evening. Soondae (Korean blood sausage) is a house favorite worth adding for $3–5. Unlimited broth and potato refills are available for a small extra charge — take them up on it.

**Price:** $ · Myeongdong Station, 9 min walk · Mon–Fri 10:00 AM – 10:00 PM, Sat until 9:00 PM, closed Sunday

![](https://places.googleapis.com/v1/places/ChIJL2dhuVajfDUR8SGlUNETu4Y/photos/AU_ZVEHrdWieXQBQVVnUq8aD2Qi2CPEBx1nzgdYQSFRQhLIjlO3LpnMoDhVtgAfX0aS0lJiLC6vlDj9K2vPEllX3bPQGL5qYL-qrtALIJIhYrlTNf5H3s_Yc9XwUGeH6NDqurQYQPCllMghlipy9HumXZ_oOcP1RPAQobnCwa_RuNTJGa8PmuVIYimCq3cmtUzrugdvjSxPv6o8LKSLh7uDq53W6gYpAHalDLBKUuwOZIQRrEbj6anEA5vOdPnjjwmT_H-KWNWjoJrI-USZr24Se6hB2gIdoOcB-BJT1u7koxEYhKczBA8XPjUxbACktf5zD1gCkkj_tSNeO8e-y-awHGq_XLDZWAR3YhRjsFaxs14lFsmzK2WkazBpepsWNY_tCI43vj7sB8i0UWQAUxxwUrf9GWWn1gGEwXBxITemwj-Ue4XgP/media?maxWidthPx=800&key=AIzaSyBE2cwdc_b0H934UaYurklIc7toYDXhGGQ)


Twelve minutes from the subway and there are already ten groups waiting by 11:45 AM on a weekday. Butai Act 2 is a small Japanese diner near Euljiro running a tight menu — mazesoba, katsu, a katsu sandwich — with the kind of focus that makes a short menu feel like a complete statement. No English menu board out front. No particular effort to flag down visitors. Which is probably why the people inside look so comfortable.

**What to order:** Mazesoba is the move — dry mixed noodles with spicy sauce, pork, egg, and vegetables, everything tossed together before you eat it, priced around $10–12. The hirekkatsu (pork fillet cutlet) costs $14–16 and is worth ordering if you're eating with someone else: tender, barely pink in the center, best with a squeeze of lemon. The katsu sandwich is a good call if you're grabbing lunch and moving on.

**Price:** $$ · Myeongdong Station, 12 min walk · Mon–Sat: 11:00 AM – 2:00 PM and 5:00 – 8:30 PM, closed Sunday

## Before You Go: A Few Practical Notes

None of these restaurants take reservations, so timing matters. For Myeongdong Kyoja and Ranju Kalmyeon, aim to arrive before noon or after 2 PM — the lunch rush hits hard between noon and 2 PM, and lines can reach 30 to 40 minutes during peak times. Butai Act 2 operates on a split schedule with a hard close between lunch and dinner, so don't show up at 3 PM expecting to get in. Dongwon is closed on Sundays, which is worth checking before heading out. Most spots are within a 5 to 12 minute walk from Myeongdong Station, so budget extra time if you're unfamiliar with the area.
