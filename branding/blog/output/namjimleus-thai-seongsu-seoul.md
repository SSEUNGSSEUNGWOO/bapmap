# Namjimleus: Best Thai Restaurant in Seongsu, Seoul

There's a staircase off a side street near Seongsu Station that takes you underground, and at the bottom of it is a Thai restaurant that I've now thought about twice since leaving. That's the test I use. Not stars, not vibes. Just whether I'm still thinking about the food.

**Namjimleus has 15 seats.** The lighting is low, the wood is dark, and someone made real decisions about the interior — it actually looks like a place you'd eat in Bangkok rather than a franchise interpretation of one. It fills up fast. I went without a reservation and waited. Don't do that.

---

## Why Visit Namjimleus

- **Gopchang adds rare depth**: Tom yum with beef intestines creates an iron-edged, fatty complexity you don't usually find in Korean noodle soups
- **Authentic Bangkok interior**: Not a themed franchise — actual design decisions that reflect real Thai dining spaces
- **Fried soft-shell crab technique**: Panang curry base with pre-fried crab delivers textural contrast (crunch before sauce)
- **Thoughtfully calibrated flavors**: Thai fundamentals executed well, with slight adjustment toward Korean palates (less aggressive heat, but broth still has backbone)

---

## What I Ate

The **Gopchang Tom Yum Rice Noodles (곱창똠얌쌀국수, ₩18,000)** are why I'd go back. Tom yum usually lives or dies on whether the broth has any backbone, and this one does. It's sour and funky in the way that actually clears your head — lemongrass and galangal doing real work — with gopchang (intestines) adding a layer of fatty, iron-edged depth that you don't usually get in a noodle soup here. The texture of the intestines is fine if you're into offal, a firm no if you're not. That's a you problem, not a them problem.

The **Soft-shell Crab Panang Curry (뿌님팟퐁커리, ₩29,500)** is the one people mention most, and for good reason. Panang is thicker and less aggressive than a standard red curry — coconut-heavy, slightly sweet, with kaffir lime doing the aromatic lifting. The soft-shell crab is fried before it goes in, so you get some crunch before the sauce takes over. It's a rich dish. One person could finish it, but you'd want something else alongside.

I also had the **Khao Kha Mu (pig's feet rice)**, which is the quieter dish on the table. Braised pork, tender enough to fall apart with light pressure, served over rice with a sauce that's more subtly spiced than you might expect. It's not the showiest thing here, but it's the kind of food you eat without talking.

## One Thing to Know

A few reviewers have mentioned that the flavors are adjusted toward Korean palates — less aggressive heat, slightly softened edges. I'd say that's partially true. This isn't a place that's going to challenge you with bird's eye chili in everything. If you're chasing maximum authenticity above all else, you might have notes. If you're chasing a well-executed meal in a basement in Seongsu, this works.

## Getting There

**Location**: Seongsu Station (Seoul Metro Line 2), Exit 4. Three-minute walk. Below street level — look for the stairs going down.

**Hours**: 
- Monday–Friday: 11:00 AM–2:30 PM, 5:00–9:00 PM
- Weekends: 12:00–3:30 PM, 5:00–9:00 PM

**Reservation**: Strongly recommended. The restaurant has only 15 seats and fills quickly. Walk-ins have no guarantee of seating.

## FAQ

**Is Namjimleus authentic Thai food?**
The restaurant uses authentic Thai techniques and flavor foundations (tom yum broth with lemongrass and galangal, Panang curry with kaffir lime). The kitchen has made deliberate adjustments toward slightly less aggressive heat to suit Korean palates, but the backbone of the cuisine is genuine.

**Do I need a reservation?**
Yes. With only 15 seats, the restaurant fills quickly and doesn't guarantee walk-in seating. Always call ahead to book.

**What's the signature dish at Namjimleus?**
The Gopchang Tom Yum Rice Noodles (₩18,000) and Soft-shell Crab Panang Curry (₩29,500) are the most notable dishes. The gopchang adds unique fatty depth, and the soft-shell crab is fried before currying for textural contrast.

**Is this restaurant good for first-time Thai food eaters?**
Yes, especially if you prefer milder heat. The flavors are intentionally adjusted toward Korean palates. However, dishes like the Gopchang Tom Yum contain offal (beef intestines), which may not suit all diners.

**How long is the wait without a reservation?**
Wait times vary, but the small 15-seat capacity means the restaurant can fill up quickly during lunch and dinner hours. Reservations are essential to avoid long waits.
