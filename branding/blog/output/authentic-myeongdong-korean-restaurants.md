Myeongdong gets a bad rap for being all corn dogs and overpriced street snacks. Fair — but if you know where to look, there are proper sit-down restaurants here that Seoulites have been going to for decades. Here’s where to actually eat.

### Myeongdong Kyoja Main Restaurant: The Knife-Cut Noodles and Dumplings That Built a Queue

![](https://places.googleapis.com/v1/places/ChIJW7FBDfCifDURHTpisLbVUH0/photos/AU_ZVEGREfrcSGD32H2ByaW_q4ewKiR0OMqgMM135-CsoKlPL9iBtEwjgmIAnDiIIy7NijuFBGdwUXx8KyumitwPPw8TwL3OPI6KQGFPjlCo66GCxQ5IzvowOWVfzGYwbXZ2nrVC_GvS74bhCLBFEJXGnJhERk0M6Yampdp8H6tP9QvcpiRrGtpzkR0wWhr6CN1TzX0aOek9byp7eR1b9Iy5tpZNaZ7jK_LIerCGiVV9dbhY2tmZBIByHGACs1-VkbfdgygUlpYiBmjz91nNO7T9ppOvGtJBVRcZ34Oaqfi-IXtRow/media?maxWidthPx=800&key=AIzaSyBE2cwdc_b0H934UaYurklIc7toYDXhGGQ)


You’ve probably walked past this place, its line a near-permanent fixture spilling onto the street. Myeongdong Kyoja (명동교자) is an institution, renowned for a concise menu since 1966 that doesn’t mess around with reinvention. Here, it’s all about execution — precision honed through years of practice. The restaurant enjoys a steady foot traffic with over 300 visitors daily.

**What to order**: Start with the Mandu (dumplings); they’re plump and steaming with flavor. Move onto the Kalguksu (knife-cut noodle soup), a warm bowl of silky noodles in garlic-heavy broth. Kongguksu (chilled soybean noodles) makes a perfect alternative during summer, offering a refreshing taste that draws locals back each season.

**Price**: Expect to spend around 10,000-12,000 KRW per person. 
**Subway**: Myeongdong Station, 3-minute walk

Myeongdong is full of tourist traps dressed up in neon. Ranju Kalmyeon (란주칼면) is not one of them. It’s famous for dosamyeon, a Chinese knife-cut noodle distinguished by its thick-but-tender texture, all hand-shaved before your eyes. Regulars have raved about the quality of their dishes for over a decade.

**What to order**: The Seafood Stir-fried Dosakmyeon is a standout — tangy, spicy, and loaded with fresh seafood. If broth is more your thing, the Samson Seafood Jjamppong Dosakmyeon serves up chewy noodles in a spicy sea of goodness, offering a taste beloved by the local community.

**Price**: Meals generally cost between 10,000 and 15,000 KRW  
**Subway**: Myeongdong Station, 5-minute walk

### Dongwon Restaurant: A Pork Spine Soup Institution That Locals Keep Coming Back To

Away from the more obvious traps lies Dongwon Restaurant (동원집), where the gamjatang (pork spine soup) tells the real story. With a location just nine minutes from the subway, it’s a favorite among those in the know, known for serving over 200 bowls of this hearty dish daily. This place offers rewards to those willing to venture just a little further out.

**What to order**: Obviously, the Gamjatang — a hearty broth surrounding fork-tender pork. Try the Soondae (Korean blood sausage) if you're feeling adventurous. Good news for the hungry: broth and potatoes have unlimited refills for a minor extra charge, making it a popular choice during chilly evenings.

**Price**: Most dishes are priced between 8,000 and 10,000 KRW  
**Subway**: Myeongdong Station, 9-minute walk  
[Bapmap Link](bapmap.com/spots/dongwon-restaurant)

You wouldn't expect to find a situation like this — no big English menu out front, and definitely not something you'll find in typical tourist digs. But by 11:45 AM, Butai Act 2 (부타이 2막) already has ten groups waiting, and for good reason; it’s a favorite for its unique menu that attracts locals in droves.

**What to order**: The Mazesoba is a must-try: cold, mixed soba with a punch of spicy sauce. Their Hirekkatsu, with its slightly pink, juicy center, goes down perfectly with a hit of lemon. For something handheld, the Katsu sandwich is crispy and indulgent, praised by regulars for its deep flavor.

**Price**: Main dishes range from 9,000 to 15,000 KRW  
**Subway**: Myeongdong Station, 12-minute walk  
[Bapmap Link](bapmap.com/spots/butai-act-2)

### Closing Tips

Each of these places can get busy, so plan your visit during off-peak hours. For Myeongdong Kyoja and Ranju Kalmyeon, odd hours like 3 PM can be your best bet to skip the line. Dongwon is best visited during the dinner rush when you can really soak in the atmosphere, which often buzzes with chatter and laughter, while Butai Act 2 is ideal for an early lunch. Keep miles away from those tourist traps. Eat like a local. Take it easy; enjoy every bite.
