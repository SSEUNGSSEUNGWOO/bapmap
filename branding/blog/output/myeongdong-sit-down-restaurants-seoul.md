# Where to Actually Eat in Myeongdong (Not Just Street Food)

Myeongdong gets a bad rap for being all corn dogs and overpriced street snacks. Fair — but if you know where to look, there are proper sit-down restaurants here that Seoulites have been going to for decades. The tourist guides send you to the pedestrian street stalls. This list does something different: it points you toward actual restaurants where people come specifically for the food, not because they stumbled in from the shopping crowds. These are places with regulars, with dishes worth sitting down for, and with price points that won't make you feel like you got taxed for being a foreigner.

---

## Myeongdong Gyoja — The bowl that built its reputation on one dish

This place has been serving the same knife-cut noodle soup since 1966, and the line outside most lunchtimes tells you everything you need to know. The kalguksu here is dense and deeply savory — the broth is slow-cooked with chicken and vegetables until it's almost silky. The dumplings (mandu) are big, tightly packed, and arrive in a steamer basket. Order both. The banchan is minimal because they don't need to distract you. Tables turn fast and the staff are efficient rather than warm, but that's the deal — you're here for the food.

**What to order:** Kalguksu (칼국수), bibim guksu if you want something cold, and the steamed mandu to share

**Price:** ₩10,000–13,000 per person | **Subway:** Myeongdong Station, Exit 8 (3-minute walk)

## Eulji OB Bear — A pojangmacha that grew up

Technically this sits just over the line into Euljiro, but it's a five-minute walk from central Myeongdong and it solves a real problem: where to actually drink and eat something good when you're done shopping. This is an old-school Korean pub — soju, draft beer, and a tight menu of anju (drinking food) that includes the kind of fried chicken and pajeon that gets better with every round. The space looks like it hasn't changed since the 1980s, which is a feature. Go after 6pm, expect noise.

**What to order:** Haemul pajeon (seafood scallion pancake), fried chicken, cold Cass or OB draft

**Price:** ₩6,000–9,000 per dish, soju ₩5,000 | **Subway:** Euljiro 1-ga Station, Exit 2

## Bukchang Donkaseu — The donkatsu that people come back for

Donkatsu — Korean-style breaded pork cutlet — gets dismissed as comfort food, which is exactly what it is and why this place works. Bukchang Donkaseu has been in the same location for over 40 years, and their version of the dish is wider, crispier, and more carefully fried than most. The sauce is applied at the table. Lunch sets come with soup and rice and land under ₩12,000. It's small, seats maybe 30, and fills up fast on weekdays. This is where people who work in the area eat lunch, which is the most reliable signal you can get.

**What to order:** Classic donkaseu set, cheese donkaseu if you want something heavier

**Price:** ₩10,000–14,000 | **Subway:** Myeongdong Station, Exit 5 (5-minute walk)

## Jungsik Original — Upscale Korean without the performance

This is the original location of the restaurant that eventually expanded to New York. The Seoul dining room is quieter and less theatrical than its reputation suggests — it's a proper sit-down meal, not a tasting menu event. The food is contemporary Korean: familiar ingredients reframed with French technique. If you want one nicer meal in Myeongdong that isn't a hotel restaurant, this is it. Book at least a week ahead. Lunch is significantly cheaper than dinner and gives you most of the same dishes.

**What to order:** Bibimbap if it's on the lunch menu, the doenjang-based soup courses, whatever the seasonal fish is

**Price:** Lunch from ₩45,000 per person, dinner from ₩120,000 | **Subway:** Myeongdong Station, Exit 8

## Gwangdong Haejang-guk — Somewhere to be at 8am

Haejang-guk is hangover soup, but framing it that way undersells it. This is a serious, mineral-rich broth with chunks of coagulated blood, vegetables, and tender beef — it's breakfast for people who need something substantial. Gwangdong has been operating early morning hours for decades and draws a mix of night workers, early commuters, and the occasional tourist who figured out that the best meal in Myeongdong might be the one you eat before the crowds arrive. Open from 7am. Cash preferred.

**What to order:** Seonji haejang-guk (blood soup), baechu-doenjang-guk if you want something milder

**Price:** ₩9,000–11,000 | **Subway:** Myeongdong Station, Exit 8

## Before you go: practical notes

Myeongdong's lunch rush runs from 12pm to 1:30pm and is genuinely brutal — lines form fast at the better-known spots. Either go before noon or after 1:30pm. For Jungsik, book online through their website at least a week out, especially for weekends. Most places on this list are cash-friendly but also accept cards; Gwangdong is the exception, so bring won. Avoid the restaurant floors inside the Lotte and Shinsegae buildings unless you specifically want a department store meal — they're fine, but you can do better on the streets just outside them.
