# Gucci Osteria Seoul da Massimo Bottura

Massimo Bottura's only restaurant in Asia sits inside the Gucci store in Apgujeong. You take an elevator up through racks of leather goods, step out into a dining room that looks like someone spent real money on the lighting, and sit down to eat food conceived by the same chef behind Osteria Francescana in Modena — three Michelin stars, the whole thing. The Seoul outpost opened in 2021 and remains the only Bottura location outside Europe. Set lunches run around ₩120,000 to ₩150,000 per person depending on what they're running that day, with dinner pushing higher. Whether or not you care about any of that context, the room will make you feel it.

Bottura's concept here is Italian cuisine inflected with local ingredients and the aesthetic priorities of wherever the restaurant lives. In Seoul, that means Korean touches folded into dishes that read as Italian in structure. The execution is careful. The food is genuinely interesting, which I say as someone who is suspicious of restaurants attached to fashion brands on principle.

The *Tortellini in Parmigiano Reggiano* — a signature dish carried over from Modena — is probably the thing to order if you order nothing else. It's pasta in a broth made almost entirely from parmesan rind. The flavor is intense in a way that reads closer to umami than dairy, and the tortellini themselves are thin enough that the filling registers as something approaching a texture rather than just mass. I thought about it afterward, which is the real test.

The Korean collaboration dishes rotate, so what I had may not be what you'll get. During my visit, one of the courses leaned into doenjang in a way that could have been a gimmick but wasn't — the fermented soybean paste used not as a garnish or a nod but as actual structural flavor in the dish. It worked. I was slightly annoyed that it worked.

Portions are not large. You know this going in, or you should. This is a tasting menu situation. Wine pairing takes the bill somewhere uncomfortable. The value question depends entirely on what you expect from a meal and whether atmosphere is part of that calculation for you. The food quality justifies a significant price. Whether it justifies *that* price is a personal call.

One honest caveat: the service is attentive in a way that occasionally tips into hovering. I was alone for about forty seconds and someone came by to check on me twice. Not a dealbreaker, but if you want space to actually talk to the person across from you, you'll notice it.

The room itself does something that fashion-brand restaurants usually get wrong — it doesn't feel like a showroom you're eating in. The design is warm. Natural light comes in well at lunch. There's art on the walls that earns being looked at. Dinner service turns the atmosphere toward something more theatrical, which is either appealing or exhausting depending on what you need that evening.

This is the one I'd bring someone who wants to impress without defaulting to a standard Michelin stop, or someone visiting Seoul who already knows the city's Korean dining scene and wants something genuinely different. It is not a representative meal of where Seoul restaurants are right now — it's an Italian chef's interpretation of Seoul filtered through a Florentine fashion house. That's a lot of layers. Somehow it holds together.

**Reservations are required.** Book through their website well in advance, especially for Friday and Saturday evenings. Walk-ins do not appear to happen.

**Getting there:** Apgujeong Rodeo Station (Bundang Line, Exit 6). About nine minutes on foot through a neighborhood where the storefronts will make you feel underdressed before you've even arrived at the Gucci building.

**Closed Mondays. Sundays lunch only until 3:00 PM.**
