# Where to Actually Eat in Myeongdong (Not Just Street Food)

Myeongdong gets a bad rap for being all corn dogs and overpriced street snacks. Fair — but if you know where to look, there are proper sit-down restaurants here that Seoulites have been going to for decades. These aren't places that opened last year to catch tourist foot traffic. They're restaurants with regulars, with menus that haven't needed reinventing, and with queues made up of people who came specifically for the food. Here's where to actually eat.

---

## 1. Myeongdong Kyoja — Knife-cut noodles and dumplings done the same way since 1966

There will be a line. There is always a line. Don't let that put you off — it moves faster than you'd expect, and what's waiting at the end earns it. Myeongdong Kyoja runs a tight, focused menu: kalguksu (knife-cut noodle soup), mandu (dumplings), and kongguksu (chilled soybean noodles, available in summer). That's essentially it. The broth is anchovy-based and clear, the dumplings are dense and well-seasoned, and the whole thing costs you less than most meals in the neighborhood. This is the kind of place where the food hasn't changed because it doesn't need to. If you're visiting in summer, the kongguksu is worth going out of your way for.

**What to order:** Kalguksu, mandu, kongguksu (summer only)
**Price:** $$
**Getting there:** Myeongdong Station, 3 min walk
**Hours:** Daily 10:30 AM – 9:00 PM
**More info:** [bapmap.com/spots/myeongdong-kyoja-main-restaurant](https://bapmap.com/spots/myeongdong-kyoja-main-restaurant)

## 2. Ranju Kalmyeon — Shanxi-style knife-cut noodles that have nothing to do with Chinese-Korean takeout

If your reference point for Chinese food in Korea is jjajangmyeon delivery, Ranju Kalmyeon is going to recalibrate things. This place serves dosamyeon — noodles made by shaving dough directly off a block with a blade, creating pieces that are thick in the middle, thin at the edges, and genuinely different in texture from anything else you'll eat on this trip. Go for the seafood stir-fried dosamyeon, which pairs that chewy noodle with fresh seafood and a sauce that has real depth. The jjamppong version is the move if you want something broth-based — crisp, spicy, and satisfying in a way that the tourist-strip alternatives aren't. Smaller review count than some spots on this list, but the regulars here are loyal for a reason.

**What to order:** Seafood Stir-fried Dosamyeon, Samson Seafood Jjamppong Dosamyeon
**Price:** $$
**Getting there:** Myeongdong Station, 5 min walk
**Hours:** Daily 11:00 AM – 9:30 PM
**More info:** [bapmap.com/spots/ranju-kalmyeon](https://bapmap.com/spots/ranju-kalmyeon)

## 3. Dongwon Restaurant — Gamjatang for people who actually want to eat like they're in Seoul

Nine minutes from the subway station is just far enough that the tourist density drops noticeably. Dongwon Restaurant has been serving gamjatang — pork spine soup — to the people who live and work around here, and it shows in the price and the portions. The broth is slow-braised and deeply savory, the pork pulls off the bone cleanly, and the potatoes absorb everything. Order the soondae (Korean blood sausage) on the side if you're open to it — it's a house favorite and worth trying if you haven't. Extra broth and potato refills are available for a small charge, which matters when you're halfway through a bowl and realize you want more. Closed Sundays, and the 9-minute walk is honest.

**What to order:** Gamjatang, soondae
**Price:** $
**Getting there:** Myeongdong Station, 9 min walk
**Hours:** Mon–Fri 10:00 AM – 10:00 PM, Sat 10:00 AM – 9:00 PM, Closed Sunday
**More info:** [bapmap.com/spots/dongwon-restaraunt](https://bapmap.com/spots/dongwon-restaraunt)

## 4. Butai Act 2 — A Japanese noodle counter near Euljiro where Koreans queue on weekday lunches

No English signage out front, split hours, closed Sundays, twelve minutes from the subway. Butai Act 2 is not trying to make it easy for you to find it — and yet by 11:45 AM on a weekday, there are already groups waiting outside. The draw is mazesoba: dry mixed noodles with spicy sauce, minced pork, egg, and fresh vegetables. You toss it yourself at the table. It's rich, textured, and the kind of bowl you think about afterward. The hirekkatsu (pork cutlet, lightly pink in the center) is worth ordering if you're hungry, and the katsu sandwich is the move if you're picking up lunch to go. Come at opening if you want to avoid the wait. Come back during dinner service if lunch doesn't work — but note the evening hours close at 8:30 PM.

**What to order:** Mazesoba, hirekkatsu, katsu sandwich
**Price:** Unlisted — expect mid-range
**Getting there:** Myeongdong Station, 12 min walk
**Hours:** Mon–Sat 11:00 AM – 2:00 PM, 5:00 – 8:30 PM, Closed Sunday
**More info:** [bapmap.com/spots/butai-act-2](https://bapmap.com/spots/butai-act-2)
